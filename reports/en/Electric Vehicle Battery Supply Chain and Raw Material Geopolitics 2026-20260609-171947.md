## 2. Global EV Battery Supply Chain Architecture

> The global EV battery supply chain funnels from geographically dispersed raw materials toward heavily concentrated Chinese processing and production hubs, creating structural fragility despite low-cost output.

### 2.1 Supply Chain Structure

The EV battery value chain spans five stages: mining, precursor processing, cell production, pack assembly, and recycling [(1)](#ref1). Mining remains distributed—lithium from Australia and Chile, cobalt from the DRC, nickel from Indonesia—but every downstream stage narrows sharply toward Chinese-controlled bottlenecks [(12)](#ref12).

**Table 2.1: Geographic Concentration by Supply Chain Stage**
| Stage | Dominant Region | Share |
|-------|----------------|-------|
| Mining | Multi-region | Distributed |
| Processing | China | ~60% |
| Cell Production | China | ~85% |
| Pack Assembly | China/NA/EU | China ~65% |
| Recycling | China/EU | China ~50% |

This "funnel" architecture means disruptions at Chinese processing hubs cascade instantly to global cell output [(5)](#ref5). China commands ~85% of cell production capacity versus 5% for North America and 7% for Europe [(8)](#ref8)[(9)](#ref9).

### 2.2 Market Concentration

Cell manufacturing is a winner-take-most oligopoly. CATL controlled roughly 40% of global EV battery market share in 2024, BYD second at ~16% [(6)](#ref6)[(7)](#ref7). Both use vertical integration—controlling mines, precursor plants, cell lines, and pack assembly—to compress costs and secure supply [(3)](#ref3).

**Table 2.2: Top Battery Manufacturers by 2024 Market Share**
| Company | Share | HQ |
|---------|-------|----|

| BYD | ~16% | China |
| LG Energy Solution | ~12% | South Korea |

China controls 21 of the 50 critical minerals designated by the US Department of Defense [(10)](#ref10). Japan and South Korea together account for ~29% of cathode material processing capacity, the only meaningful non-Chinese midstream players [(11)](#ref11). CATL's $1.2 billion Indonesia plant, breaking ground in 2025, extends vertical integration into nickel-rich cathode production [(4)](#ref4).

The architecture delivers the lowest-cost batteries ever produced, but any geopolitical shock or natural disaster at Chinese chokepoints could freeze global EV production [(2)](#ref2)[(12)](#ref12).

## 3. Critical Raw Material Geopolitics

> The geography of critical raw materials is defined by extreme concentration — a handful of nations control the bulk of reserves, processing capacity, and export channels, creating systemic vulnerabilities for import-dependent economies.

### 3.1 Material Reserves & Production

Critical mineral supply chains exhibit a stark concentration pattern. China dominates graphite mining at ~80% of global output [(1)](#ref1) and controls over 90% of rare earth processing alongside ~80% of battery chemical refining capacity [(2)](#ref2). The DRC supplies the majority of cobalt globally [(4)](#ref4), while Indonesia holds ~22% of nickel reserves [(5)](#ref5). This clustering in a narrow set of jurisdictions creates fragility across downstream industries from electronics to defense.

| Material | Dominant Producer | Share | Source |
|----------|------------------|-------|--------|
| Graphite mining | China | ~80% | [(1)](#ref1) |
| Rare earth processing | China | >90% | [(2)](#ref2) |
| Battery chemicals | China | ~80% | [(2)](#ref2) |
| Cobalt | DRC | Majority | [(4)](#ref4) |
| Nickel reserves | Indonesia | ~22% | [(5)](#ref5) |

The concentration directly translates into extreme import dependency for consumer nations. The US relies on imports for over 90% of its gallium, graphite, indium, and bismuth supply [(3)](#ref3). Lithium offers a more nuanced picture: Argentina and Chile dominate 97% of US lithium imports [(7)](#ref7), yet emerging geothermal extraction technology in the US promises to diversify domestic supply over the medium term [(6)](#ref6).

| Material | US Import Reliance | Source |
|----------|-------------------|--------|
| Gallium | >90% | [(3)](#ref3) |
| Graphite | >90% | [(3)](#ref3) |
| Indium | >90% | [(3)](#ref3) |
| Bismuth | >90% | [(3)](#ref3) |
| Lithium (via Argentina/Chile) | 97% of imports | [(7)](#ref7) |

### 3.2 Geopolitical Control Points

Control over processing infrastructure amplifies the leverage of reserve ownership. China's December 2024 export restrictions on gallium and germanium [(8)](#ref8) and its long-standing rare earth export controls against Japan [(10)](#ref10) demonstrate how supply chain dominance converts into geopolitical pressure. These actions signal that critical minerals are now instruments of statecraft, not merely commercial commodities.

Export restrictions are escalating globally as resource nationalism spreads. Indonesia's nickel export ban forced downstream processing investment by compelling buyers to build smelters domestically [(9)](#ref9), reshaping the nickel trade's structure. The US-EU Critical Minerals Pact of 2026 represents a collective response aimed at building alternative supply corridors through friend-shoring and joint investment [(12)](#ref12). China's broader strategy goes beyond geopolitics toward sustained industrial supremacy across the entire mineral value chain [(11)](#ref11).

| Geopolitical Action | Initiator | Target | Period | Source |
|--------------------|-----------|--------|--------|--------|
| Gallium / germanium export ban | China | Global | Dec 2024 | [(8)](#ref8) |
| Rare earth export controls | China | Japan | 2010+ | [(10)](#ref10) |
| Nickel ore export ban | Indonesia | Global | 2020+ | [(9)](#ref9) |
| US-EU Critical Minerals Pact | US / EU | Mutual | 2026 | [(12)](#ref12) |

Resource nationalism has migrated from a niche concern to a central feature of great-power competition. Import-dependent economies face a stark choice: accelerate supply-chain diversification through domestic processing investment and international partnerships, or accept growing strategic vulnerability in the clean-energy and defense sectors.
